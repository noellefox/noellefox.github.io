Project Goals

The aim of our project is to conduct a deeper analysis of MLB team payroll spending and how it correlates with overall team success. We will investigate whether higher spending is directly associated with better winloss records, or if certain teams are achieving success despite smaller budgets. From there, we will analyze which player statistics teams appear to prioritize when allocating funds and evaluate whether these investments have translated into improved performance on the field.

Project Datasets

To explore the relationship between MLB payroll spending and team success, we are using multiple datasets that capture both team-level and player-level information. Together these datasets will allow us to analyze how much each team spends on its roster, how that spending translates to on-field performance, and which specific player statistics teams appear to prioritize when allocating their funds. By combining these data sources, we will be able to uncover patterns that link financial investments to player performance and overall team outcomes.

Dataset 1: Team payrolls and performance This dataset contains each MLN teams total payroll for a given season (from 2011 to 2024) along with their corresponding win-loss record. We chose this dataset because it will serve as the foundation for our analysis in that it allows us to test our initial question Do teams that spend more money actually perform better?

Question: Does higher team payroll correlate with greater success, as measured by total wins/losses in a season?

Statistics we can pull: average team payroll per season, top-performing teams per dollar spent, correlation coefficient between payroll and win percentage.

Potential graph: We can create a scatter plot with team payroll on the x-axis and win percentage on the y-axis. We could incorporate a regression line to visualize the relationship between spending and success

Dataset 2: Player Performance Statistics The dataset includes detailed player-level statistics such as batting average, on-base percentage, home runs, and other relevant performance metrics. It provides an understanding of how individual players contribute to team outcomes. We will use this dataset to explore what specific player characteristics or skills teams might be rewarding with higher salaries or greater investment. By linking this dataset with our third on player salaries, we can see what traits teams value more from a spending perspective.

Question: Which player statistics appear to be most values by teams when determining payroll allocation?

Statistics we can pull: top performing players, correlation between player wins and salary, and average performing metrics for top vs. bottom paid players.

Potential graph: a correlation heatmap to compare player stats vs salary. We can make the rows and columns of player performance metrics and compare them to their salary.

Dataset 3: Player Team and Salary This dataset includes each players name, their team affiliation, and their corresponding yearly salary. This connects player-level earnings to both team payroll and individual performance. We are utilizing this because it will serve as the bridge between our payroll and performance datasets. This will allow us to explore how traits are being valued in terms of spending and if this is contributing to overall team success.

Question: How do individual player salaries vary across teams, and do higher-paid players influence a teams success?

Statistics we can pull: average salary per team per position, salary distribution across the league, top highest players, and percentage of payroll contributed to the top earners of the team

Potential graph: A stacked bar chart showing each teams total payroll broken down by top-paid versus average player.

By using all three of these datasets we will be able to evaluate the relationship between financial investment and team success across multiple perspectives. The combined data will allow us to determine whether higher payrolls truly lead to stronger team performance and which specific player metrics drive salary and spending decisions. Furthermore we can uncover trends as to traits teams value the most, as reflected by their salary, and if these traits are contributing to team success.

#clean the data ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

#only keep necessary columns
to_drop = ['Average Age', 'Active 26-Man', 'Injured', 'Retained', 'Buried', 'Postseason']   #columns to drop
df = df.drop(columns=to_drop, errors='ignore')      # drop the columns from the dataframe

#change payroll column from string to type float for efficient use later
df['Total Payroll Allocations'] = (
    df['Total Payroll Allocations']
      .astype(str)
      .str.replace(r'[\$,]', '', regex=True) #gets rid of dollar sign and
      .astype(float)
)


#change payroll column to be in millions to avoid scientific notation
df["Payroll_M"] = df["Total Payroll Allocations"] / 1_000_000
